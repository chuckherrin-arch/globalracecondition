The American Technate

A continental self-sufficiency model from 1930s America, and why a map from 1940 looks like a policy briefing from 2026.

The Map

The red zone covers the entire United States, all of Canada, Greenland, Central America, the Caribbean, Cuba, and parts of Colombia, Venezuela, and the Guyanas. Defense bases ring the perimeter from the Aleutians to Bermuda to the Galapagos. The legend reads: "Boundary of the Technate" and "Defense Bases." The scale is continental. The ambition is hemispheric self-sufficiency.

This map was published 86 years ago. Compare it to the territorial signals of the current US administration: Greenland acquisition pressure, Canadian "51st state" rhetoric, Monroe Doctrine reassertion in Venezuela, border militarization as a southern security perimeter, and Caribbean/Central American economic leverage. The overlap is striking.

Origins: The Technocracy Movement

The Technocracy movement was founded by Howard Scott in 1919 as the Technical Alliance in New York. Its members were mostly scientists and engineers who believed the Depression proved that politicians and financiers were incompetent to manage an industrial economy. Their proposal: replace the "price system" (money-based economics) with energy accounting, measuring all economic value in energy units rather than currency. Engineers and scientists would govern a continental resource zone, not elected politicians.

In 1932, Scott and Columbia University professor Walter Rautenstrauch formed the Committee on Technocracy at Columbia, which gained national attention as an alternative to the failing economic order. By 1933 the movement had splintered, with Scott leading Technocracy Incorporated. At its peak, the organization had over 500,000 members in California alone.

Key Concepts

Energy Certificates: Citizens would receive equal annual allotments of energy-denominated scrip, expiring at year-end to prevent capital accumulation. The economy would be managed by technical allocation of energy resources, not market pricing. This is not communism (no collective ownership of production) and not capitalism (no market pricing). It is governance by thermodynamic accounting.

The Continental Unit: The Technate was defined not by national borders but by resource logic. The natural resources and geography of North America, from Panama to the North Pole, make it an independent, self-sustaining unit. The movement's founders argued that governing this unit required continental-scale planning that nation-state boundaries made impossible.

The Energy Survey of North America: Beginning in the 1920s, Scott and his colleagues conducted an inventory of every energy input and output on the continent, establishing a value for all goods and services by measuring the energy required to produce them. This was the empirical foundation for the claim that energy, not money, is the true unit of economic value.

The Haldeman Connection

Joshua Haldeman, Elon Musk's maternal grandfather, was a Technocracy leader in Saskatchewan from 1936 to 1941. He was arrested and charged with sedition for the movement's anti-democratic organizing during wartime. He left the movement after concluding it had become treasonous, went on to found his own political party, and later joined the Social Credit movement in Alberta.

This is not a claim of direct policy lineage. It is a historical data point worth noting when the most influential technologist in the current administration has a direct family connection to the movement that drew the map.

The Energy-AI Parallel

The Technocracy movement argued in the 1930s that energy, not money, is the binding constraint on economic capacity. In 2026, this thesis has unexpected resonance: AI infrastructure requires massive energy inputs. Data center siting, power grid capacity, and energy sovereignty are now binding constraints on national AI capability. Google is acquiring natural gas capacity. Microsoft is restarting nuclear plants. Hyperscalers are competing for power purchase agreements measured in gigawatts.

A Technate built on energy self-sufficiency would also be an AI self-sufficiency zone. Whether this parallel is coincidence or signal is an open question, but US energy policy increasingly treats AI compute capacity as a strategic resource: executive orders on AI infrastructure, CHIPS Act industrial policy, and natural gas expansion all point in this direction.

How GRC Uses This Framework

The Technate concept is used in Global Race Condition briefings as a behavioral pattern-matcher, not as a claim about conscious strategy. The analytical question is simple: when a US action appears irrational through the lens of maintaining global hegemony, does it become coherent through the lens of hemispheric self-sufficiency?

The framework is most useful when applied alongside Dugin's Russian strategic framework. When both lenses predict the same observable outcome from different causal chains (the "mirror test"), that convergence is analytically significant regardless of whether either framework accurately describes the actors' conscious intent.

The framework is weakest when used as a universal explainer. Some tariffs are just bad economics. Some provocations are just ego. The test is whether a pattern of consistent actions across multiple domains matches the Technate template, not whether any single action does.

Modern Analytical Layer

Game-theoretic analysis has been applied to the Technate pattern, framing recent US behavior as deliberate hemispheric consolidation rather than incoherence. The modern contribution is the structural framing: if a great power concluded that the existing global financial system was unsustainable, controlled collapse toward regional self-sufficiency would be the rational play. This overlay provides explanatory power for actions that appear self-defeating through conventional alliance-based analysis. The behavioral observations are assessed independently of any specific analyst's institutional position or national perspective.
