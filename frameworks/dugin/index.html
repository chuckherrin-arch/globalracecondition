Dugin's Russian Strategic Framework

"Foundations of Geopolitics" (1997): the textbook that shaped a generation of Russian military and intelligence leadership.

What It Is

Aleksandr Dugin's "Foundations of Geopolitics" was published in 1997 and has been used as a textbook at Russia's General Staff Academy, the institution that trains Russia's senior military and intelligence officers. This is not a fringe manifesto. It is a published doctrinal framework that has been taught to the people who make Russian strategic decisions for over two decades.

The book builds on classical geopolitical theory, particularly Halford Mackinder's "Heartland" thesis: whoever controls the Eurasian landmass controls the world. Dugin updates this for the post-Cold War era, arguing that Russia's survival depends on building a Eurasian empire that can counter the US-led Atlantic maritime order.

Core Doctrine

Land Power vs Sea Power

History, in Dugin's framework, is a permanent contest between continental land powers (tellurocracies: Russia, Germany, Iran historically) and maritime/coastal powers (thalassocracies: the US, UK, Japan). Russia is the "Heartland." The US-led Atlantic bloc is the primary adversary. Russia's strategic imperative: consolidate control of the Eurasian landmass and break the Atlantic encirclement.

The New Empire

Russia cannot survive as a mere nation-state. It requires a Eurasian empire built on four pillars: the Russian core, a European pillar anchored by Germany, a Pacific pillar anchored by Japan, and an Islamic pillar anchored by Iran. Integration is based on civilizational identity and shared opposition to Atlantic dominance, not ethnicity alone.

Strategic Axes

Moscow-Berlin: Germany anchors a European bloc independent of US control. A Franco-German alliance is the "spinal column" of a New Europe oriented toward Moscow, not Washington.

Moscow-Tokyo: Japan as the eastern anchor of Eurasia. Dugin explicitly warns against a deep Russian-Chinese alliance, arguing it would alienate Japan and create a rival.

Moscow-Tehran: Iran as the anchor of an Islamic civilizational bloc. Islam is framed as "naturally" anti-Atlantic. Priorities: strengthen Iranian influence, weaken Turkish secularism (pro-Atlantic), co-opt pan-Arab movements.

Regional Prescriptions

Ukraine: "A sovereign Ukraine is a direct geopolitical threat to Russia." This is the single most emphasized prescription in the entire book. Written in 1997, over two decades before the 2022 invasion.

The United States: The primary adversary. Strategy: encourage internal fragmentation along racial, cultural, and regional lines. Support isolationism. Foster distrust between the US and its European and Pacific allies. The goal is not military defeat but geopolitical irrelevance: an America too internally divided to project power into Eurasia.

The Baltic States: Prevent NATO integration. Exploit Russian-speaking populations as leverage.

Turkey: Weaken its secular, NATO-aligned orientation. Encourage its Eurasian/Islamic identity over its Atlantic identity.

China: Treated with caution, not as an ally. Dugin warns that a strong, unified China is a potential rival. He recommends leveraging Tibet, Xinjiang, and Manchuria as pressure points. A full Sino-Russian alliance is explicitly not Dugin's prescription, which diverges significantly from current Russian policy.

How GRC Uses This Framework

The briefings use Dugin's framework as a pattern-matching checklist for observed Russian actions. The question is always: "Does this action make more sense when viewed through Dugin's strategic lens than through a purely reactive or opportunistic model?"

When Russian actions diverge from Dugin's prescriptions (e.g., the deep China alignment he warned against), that divergence is itself analytically significant. It suggests doctrinal evolution, pragmatic compromise, or internal strategic disagreement.

Dugin is influential but not the sole voice. Competing schools exist: Primakov's multipolarity, Surkov's "sovereign democracy," and pragmatic Atlanticists who existed before 2014. The framework is cited as "Dugin's framework," not as "Russian strategy" monolithically.

The Dual-Power Framework

GRC applies Dugin's framework alongside the American Technate concept as a dual-power analytical lens. The most valuable finding occurs when both frameworks predict the same observable outcome from different causal chains: when "Russia is executing its strategy" and "America is executing its strategy" produce identical observations, the reader needs to know, because the causal attribution matters enormously for prediction even when the current observation looks the same.
